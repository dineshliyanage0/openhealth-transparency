# OpenHealth Transparency (OHT)

## Project Rationale and Motivation Document

## 1. Introduction

OpenHealth Transparency (OHT) is an open-source initiative primarily focused on addressing a **real and pressing issue within the Sri Lankan public healthcare system**.

Sri Lanka provides free public healthcare to its citizens, and this system plays a critical role in ensuring equitable access to medical services. However, in practice, there are recurring situations where patients are unable to access timely treatment due to the reported unavailability of critical medical equipment.

This document outlines the **rationale**, **motivation**, and **intent** behind developing a system specifically targeted at improving **transparency and accountability within Sri Lanka’s public hospital network**.

While the system may be adapted for use in other countries, the **primary design, motivation, and initial implementation are grounded in the Sri Lankan context**.

## 2. Problem Context

Sri Lanka’s public healthcare system is designed to provide free medical services through government hospitals. Despite this, patients frequently encounter situations where:

- Critical devices (e.g., MRI, CT scan, surgical equipment) are reported as unavailable
- Equipment is said to be under repair or non-functional
- There is no clear visibility into when the device will be restored
- Patients are left without alternatives within the public system

From a system perspective, there is **no centralized, transparent mechanism** to verify or track these conditions.

As a result:

- Patients cannot validate whether a device is genuinely unavailable
- There is no publicly accessible record of downtime
- Oversight is limited due to lack of structured data

This creates a significant **information gap within Sri Lanka’s healthcare system**, directly affecting patient outcomes and public trust.

## 3. Core Problem Definition

The fundamental problem is not simply equipment failure.

The real problem is the **absence of a transparent, verifiable, and structured system** to:

- Record device availability
- Track breakdowns and repairs
- Validate claims with evidence
- Provide controlled public visibility
- Maintain accountability through audit trails

Without such a system, even genuine issues cannot be distinguished from miscommunication, inefficiencies, or misuse.

## 4. Motivation Behind the Project

This project is driven by **direct, real-world experiences within Sri Lanka’s healthcare system**.

The motivation is not theoretical — it is based on observing and experiencing situations where:

- Patients are delayed or redirected due to unavailable equipment
- There is no way to verify the actual status of medical devices
- Information is fragmented and not accessible to the public

This creates a strong need for a system that introduces:

### 4.1 Transparency in Sri Lanka’s Public Healthcare

Patients should have access to reliable information about the availability of critical medical equipment in government hospitals.

### 4.2 Accountability Within the System

A structured, auditable system ensures that equipment status, downtime, and repair processes are properly recorded and visible.

### 4.3 Data-Driven Improvements

By capturing structured data across hospitals in Sri Lanka, the system can support:

- Better policy decisions
- Faster response to equipment failures
- Identification of systemic issues across regions

## 5. Why a Technology Solution?

The problem is fundamentally an **information systems problem**.

Manual processes, fragmented records, and informal communication cannot provide:

- Real-time visibility
- Consistent verification
- Reliable historical data
- Scalable oversight

A well-designed digital platform can:

- Standardize data collection
- Enforce workflows
- Provide real-time updates
- Maintain audit trails
- Enable controlled public access

## 6. Why Open Source?

This project is intentionally developed as an open-source initiative for the following reasons:

### 6.1 Trust Through Transparency

A transparency system must itself be transparent. Open source ensures that:

- The logic is inspectable
- The data flows are understandable
- The system is not a black box

### 6.2 Community Collaboration

Healthcare transparency is a multi-disciplinary challenge. Open source allows contributions from:

- Engineers
- Healthcare professionals
- Policy experts
- Researchers

### 6.3 Adaptability

Different countries and regions face similar challenges. An open-source system can be adapted and reused across contexts.

### 6.4 Independence

Open source reduces reliance on a single organization and enables long-term sustainability.

## 7. What We Are Building

OpenHealth Transparency is designed as a **modular digital platform** consisting of:

### 7.1 Device Registry

A centralized system to record all critical medical devices across hospitals.

### 7.2 Status Tracking System

A structured mechanism to track whether devices are operational, under maintenance, or unavailable.

### 7.3 Incident Reporting System

A workflow to report issues with supporting evidence.

### 7.4 Verification Workflow

A controlled process to validate reported issues before they become publicly visible.

### 7.5 Public Transparency Portal

A platform where verified information is accessible to citizens.

### 7.6 Audit and Accountability Layer

A complete history of actions, changes, and approvals.

## 8. What This System Is NOT

It is important to clearly define boundaries.

This system:

- Does not make accusations
- Does not replace legal or regulatory processes
- Does not assume intent or misconduct
- Does not publish unverified claims as facts

Its purpose is to provide **structured, evidence-based visibility**.

## 9. Design Philosophy

The system is built on the following principles:

### 9.1 Evidence First

No information should be treated as verified without supporting data.

### 9.2 Role-Based Responsibility

Different users have clearly defined roles and permissions.

### 9.3 Auditability

Every action is traceable.

### 9.4 Controlled Transparency

Only verified and approved data is made public.

### 9.5 Neutrality

The system focuses on facts, not narratives.

## 10. Expected Impact

If implemented effectively, the system can:

- Improve patient awareness of healthcare availability
- Reduce uncertainty in accessing services
- Strengthen accountability mechanisms
- Enable data-driven policy decisions
- Highlight systemic inefficiencies
- Improve maintenance and repair response times

## 11. Long-Term Vision

The immediate focus of this project is Sri Lanka.

The goal is to create a **practical, working system that addresses real challenges within Sri Lanka’s public healthcare infrastructure**.

If successful, the platform may later be adapted for:

- Other countries with similar public healthcare challenges
- Regional healthcare transparency initiatives

However, the priority remains:

**Solving a real, present problem within Sri Lanka first.**

## 12. Conclusion

OpenHealth Transparency is not just a software project.

It is an attempt to solve a fundamental problem in public systems: the lack of **structured, verifiable, and accessible information**.

By combining technology, transparency, and accountability, this initiative aims to create a system where trust is built not on assumptions, but on **visible, traceable, and evidence-backed data**.
